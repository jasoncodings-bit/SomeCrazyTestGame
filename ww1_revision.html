Memory Tip: Use the acronym MAIN to remember the four long-term causes: Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism.

Militarism

  • Militarism means the belief that a nation should maintain a strong military and be prepared to use it.
  • Between 1870 and 1914, all major European powers massively increased the size of their armies and navies.
  • Britain and Germany competed in a naval arms race — both built large battleships called "Dreadnoughts".
  • By 1914, Germany had the strongest army in Europe. Military thinking dominated government decisions.
  • War was widely seen as glorious and manly — this made nations more willing to fight.

Alliances

  • European powers formed military alliances — agreements to defend each other if attacked.
  • Triple Alliance (1882): Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy agreed to support each other.
  • Triple Entente (1907): Britain, France, and Russia formed a looser agreement for mutual support.
  • These alliances meant that a small conflict between two nations could rapidly drag in all of Europe — a "chain reaction" effect.
  • When Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia in 1914, Russia mobilised, Germany declared war on Russia, France entered, then Britain entered when Germany invaded Belgium.

Imperialism

  • Imperialism is when one nation extends its power by colonising or controlling other territories.
  • By 1914, Britain controlled about 25% of the world's land through its empire.
  • Germany, a newer nation, wanted more colonies and felt "left out" — this created resentment and rivalry.
  • Competition for colonies in Africa and Asia caused several crises before 1914 (e.g., the Moroccan Crises of 1905 and 1911).

Nationalism

  • Nationalism is strong pride in and loyalty to one's nation or ethnic group, and the desire for independence.
  • In the Balkans ("the powder keg of Europe"), ethnic groups like Serbs, Bulgarians, and Greeks wanted independence from the Austro-Hungarian and Ottoman Empires.
  • Serbia wanted to unite all South Slavic peoples under one nation, which directly threatened Austria-Hungary.
  • The Spark: On 28 June 1914, Archduke Franz Ferdinand (heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne) was assassinated in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip, a member of the Black Hand — a Serbian nationalist group.
  • Austria-Hungary blamed Serbia and issued an ultimatum. When Serbia did not fully comply, Austria-Hungary declared war.
Exam Focus: You need to explain EACH cause, not just name them. The best answers link the causes together — for example, explaining how alliances made nationalism in the Balkans a global crisis.
Key Fact: WW1 began on 28 July 1914 when Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia. By August 1914, most of Europe was at war.

What Was the Western Front?

  • The Western Front was the main battle zone in Western Europe, running approximately 700 km from the North Sea coast of Belgium through France to Switzerland.
  • Germany's plan (the Schlieffen Plan) was to quickly defeat France and then fight Russia. It failed — France held firm.
  • Both sides dug trenches to protect themselves, creating a line of fortification that barely moved for four years.
  • The result was a stalemate — neither side could break through the other's defences.

Trench Warfare and Conditions

  • Mud and flooding: Trenches often filled with water and mud. Soldiers could not escape the wet for weeks. This caused "trench foot" — a condition where feet rot from constant moisture.
  • Rats: Massive rats lived in the trenches, feeding on corpses and food supplies. Soldiers described rats as big as cats.
  • Lice: Lice infested soldiers' clothing and caused intense itching and disease ("trench fever").
  • Disease: Dysentery, typhoid, trench fever, and influenza spread rapidly in the cramped, unsanitary conditions.
  • Shell shock: Constant artillery bombardment caused severe psychological trauma — trembling, inability to speak or function. Today we call this PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder). At the time, soldiers were often accused of cowardice.
  • Constant danger: Snipers, artillery, poison gas, and random bombardment meant soldiers were never safe, even behind the front line.

No Man's Land

  • The open ground between the two opposing trench lines was called "No Man's Land."
  • It was filled with barbed wire, shell craters, mud, and the bodies of dead soldiers.
  • Going "over the top" — climbing out of the trenches to attack across No Man's Land — was ordered by commanders and almost always resulted in enormous casualties.

Key Battles

  • Battle of the Somme (July–November 1916): On the first day alone, the British suffered approximately 57,470 casualties (killed, wounded, missing). Total casualties for both sides exceeded 1 million.
  • Battle of Verdun (February–December 1916): France vs. Germany — approximately 700,000 combined casualties. One of the longest and bloodiest battles in history.
Exam Focus: When describing conditions, use specific details — trench foot, shell shock, rats, lice, No Man's Land. Vague answers like "it was hard" will not score well. Use examples from specific battles to show depth.
Common Mistake: Do not confuse the Western Front (France/Belgium) with Gallipoli (Ottoman Turkey). They were completely different campaigns.

Background

  • Gallipoli was a campaign planned by Winston Churchill (First Lord of the Admiralty) to open a new front and knock the Ottoman Empire out of the war.
  • The goal was to capture Istanbul (Constantinople), control the Dardanelles strait, supply Russia, and potentially end the war faster.
  • On 25 April 1915, Allied forces — including Australian and New Zealand troops (ANZACs) — landed on the Gallipoli Peninsula in Ottoman Turkey.
  • The ANZACs landed at the wrong beach (Ari Burnu, later known as ANZAC Cove) instead of a flat beach. They faced steep, rugged cliffs and immediately came under heavy fire.

How Did Soldiers View the Campaign?

Different groups experienced and viewed Gallipoli in different ways:

  • Initial enthusiasm: Many young Australian men had enlisted with excitement and a sense of adventure. They believed the war would be short and glorious.
  • Shock on landing: The reality of landing under fire at the wrong location, with Ottoman forces firing from above, was devastating. Thousands were killed or wounded in the first hours.
  • Fear and horror: Soldiers described the chaos — bodies in the water, friends dying beside them, the screams of the wounded.
  • Bravery and determination: Despite the horror, soldiers pressed forward. Acts of incredible courage were common — including soldiers carrying wounded under fire.
  • Mateship: The shared suffering created incredibly strong bonds between soldiers. Looking after your mate became central to ANZAC culture.
  • Harsh conditions: Soldiers endured extreme heat, inadequate water supplies, disease (especially dysentery), swarms of flies, and constant sniper fire for months.
  • Disillusionment: As the campaign dragged on with no progress, many soldiers and officers grew frustrated with poor planning. Letters home (when not censored) revealed deep cynicism.
  • Aboriginal soldiers: Many Indigenous Australians also served at Gallipoli — their contributions are increasingly recognised.
Key Quote: General Sir Ian Hamilton (Allied Commander) described the landing as "a feat of arms which history will recognise as one of the most daring and difficult undertaken in modern war." However, many historians argue the campaign was poorly planned from the start.
Exam Focus: "Perspectives" means you need to show different points of view. Discuss at least TWO perspectives — e.g., the initial enthusiasm vs. later disillusionment, or soldiers vs. commanders. Use specific details.

Military Outcome: A Failure

  • The campaign was a clear military failure. The Allies never broke through the Ottoman defences.
  • The evacuation was carried out in December 1915 — one of the few successful parts of the whole campaign.
  • Australian casualties: approximately 8,709 killed and 19,441 wounded.
  • Total Allied casualties (including British, French, New Zealand, Indian): over 100,000 killed.

Why It Is Significant for Australia

  • Birth of national identity: Gallipoli is widely considered the moment Australia "came of age" as a nation. Although federation occurred in 1901, Gallipoli gave Australians a shared national story based on sacrifice and courage.
  • The ANZAC Legend: The campaign gave birth to the ANZAC legend — the idea that Australian and New Zealand soldiers possessed unique qualities: courage under fire, resilience, larrikinism (a disregard for authority), and above all, mateship.
  • Australian identity vs. British identity: Gallipoli helped Australians see themselves as distinct from the British. Some felt British commanders (especially Generals Hamilton and Birdwood) had wasted Australian lives through arrogance and poor planning.
  • ANZAC Day (25 April): The date of the landing became ANZAC Day — now Australia's most important national day of commemoration. The first official ANZAC Day was observed on 25 April 1916.
  • Impact on conscription debate: The heavy losses at Gallipoli made conscription (forced military service) a major political issue in Australia. The government held two referendums in 1916 and 1917 — both were defeated.
Key Argument: Gallipoli was a military failure but a cultural triumph. It failed in its strategic goal but succeeded in creating a powerful national identity for Australia.
Exam Focus: Always distinguish between the military significance (failed) and the cultural/national significance (hugely important). The best answers address both sides.

What Is Propaganda?

  • Propaganda is biased or misleading information deliberately created and spread to promote a particular political cause, manipulate public opinion, or influence people's behaviour.
  • Propaganda is not necessarily false — it can include true information presented in a selective, misleading, or emotional way.
  • During WW1, all warring governments used propaganda on a massive scale for the first time in history.

Purposes of WW1 Propaganda

  • Recruitment: Encouraging men to enlist. Posters showed soldiers as heroes and made men who did not enlist feel ashamed. Famous examples include "Your Country Needs You" (British) and similar Australian posters.
  • Demonising the enemy: Portraying Germany as barbaric, uncivilised, and a threat to women, children, and Christian values. The word "Hun" was used as a derogatory term for Germans.
  • Maintaining civilian morale: Keeping the public supportive of the war effort, especially as casualties mounted and the war dragged on.
  • Encouraging women to pressure men: The "white feather campaign" — women handed white feathers (symbols of cowardice) to men not in uniform, publicly shaming them into enlisting.
  • Encouraging sacrifice: Convincing people to accept rationing, buy war bonds, and contribute to the war economy.

Methods of Propaganda

  • Posters: Mass produced and displayed publicly — the most visible form of WW1 propaganda.
  • Newspapers: Editors allowed (and were sometimes directed by) governments to avoid negative war coverage. Stories of German atrocities were often exaggerated.
  • Public speeches and rallies: Politicians and community leaders urged support for the war.
  • Films: Short documentary films were made showing "heroic" battle footage, often staged.
  • Schools: Children were taught patriotism and the righteousness of the Allied cause.

Connection Between Propaganda and Censorship

  • Propaganda (pushing positive messages) and censorship (blocking negative information) worked together.
  • Soldiers' letters were censored — any mention of heavy casualties, low morale, or criticism of commanders was removed.
  • The public had very little access to accurate information about the true cost of the war until after it ended.
Exam Focus: A strong answer will explain BOTH what propaganda is AND give specific examples of how it was used. Link propaganda to censorship for higher marks.

Context: Women Before WW1

  • Before WW1, Australian women had very limited roles outside the home. Most work outside the home was socially discouraged for married women.
  • However, Australian women had already won the right to vote federally in 1902 — making Australia one of the first nations to do so.

Nursing

  • The Australian Army Nursing Service (AANS) sent over 2,500 nurses to serve in the war.
  • Nurses served in Egypt, on hospital ships, at Gallipoli, on the Western Front, and in other theatres of war.
  • They worked in extremely dangerous conditions, often within range of enemy artillery, dealing with horrific wounds, amputations, and gas injuries.
  • Nursing was seen as an acceptable role for women because it was considered "caring" — but the reality was gruelling professional medical work.

Work on the Home Front

  • As millions of men enlisted, women stepped into their jobs. This was a major social change.
  • Women worked in munitions factories (making shells, guns, and chemicals), on farms, in public transport (as tram conductors and ticket collectors), in banks, offices, and government.
  • Women's organisations raised enormous sums of money for the war effort and produced goods (socks, bandages, food parcels) for soldiers.

Controversy: The White Feather Campaign

  • Some women participated in the "white feather campaign," handing white feathers (symbolising cowardice) to men not in uniform.
  • This was controversial — feathers were sometimes given to men who were injured veterans, working in vital civilian roles, or had been conscientiously rejected by the army.

Long-Term Impact

  • Women's wartime contributions directly challenged the idea that women were incapable of skilled work or public life.
  • After the war, women's suffrage movements gained strength across the world (e.g., British women over 30 gained the vote in 1918).
  • However, most Australian women were pushed back into domestic roles after the war as men returned to their jobs.
Exam Focus: Do not just list what women did — analyse the IMPACT. How did their roles challenge traditional gender expectations? What changed after the war and what did not?

How the War Ended

  • By late 1918, Germany's military was collapsing. Allied offensives pushed the Germans back along the Western Front.
  • Revolution broke out in Germany; Kaiser Wilhelm II abdicated on 9 November 1918.
  • An Armistice (an agreement to stop fighting) was signed on 11 November 1918 at 11:00 am — "the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month."
  • The Treaty of Versailles formally ended the war on 28 June 1919. It:
    • Placed full blame for the war on Germany ("War Guilt Clause" — Article 231).
    • Forced Germany to pay reparations of 132 billion gold marks.
    • Stripped Germany of its colonies and 13% of its territory.
    • Limited Germany's military to 100,000 soldiers.

The Human Cost to Australia

  • Australia sent approximately 416,000 men to war — all volunteers (conscription was never introduced).
  • Over 60,000 Australians were killed and approximately 156,000 were wounded, gassed, or taken prisoner.
  • Australia had one of the highest proportional casualty rates of any Allied nation — nearly 65% of those who served became casualties.

How WW1 Changed Australia

  • National identity: The ANZAC legend became the foundation of Australian identity — a sense that Australians were tough, loyal, and resourceful. This is still central to how Australians see themselves today.
  • Political independence: Australia signed the Treaty of Versailles as an independent signatory for the first time, not just as part of the British Empire — a significant step toward full sovereignty.
  • Domestic division: The conscription debate (1916 and 1917 referendums) deeply divided Australian society. Prime Minister Billy Hughes split the Labor Party over the issue.
  • Social change: Women's wartime roles accelerated changes in gender expectations. Returned soldiers brought back psychological and physical trauma that society was ill-equipped to deal with.
  • Economic impact: War debt and the loss of skilled workers affected the economy. The boom conditions in some industries (e.g., wool, wheat exports) declined after the war.
  • Immigration and society: Anti-German sentiment during the war led to discrimination against German-Australians; German place names in South Australia were changed.
Key Argument: WW1 was the defining moment of modern Australian nationhood. It gave Australia an independent sense of identity, a new relationship with Britain, and a cultural heritage (the ANZAC legend) that still shapes the country today.
Exam Focus: "How did it change Australia?" requires you to cover MULTIPLE areas — identity, politics, society, economy. Use specific facts and figures (e.g., 60,000 killed, two failed referendums, signed Treaty separately).

Key Dates Timeline

Memorise these dates — they come up in every exam and quiz.

1882
Triple Alliance Formed

Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy sign a mutual defence agreement — one of the long-term causes of WW1.

1907
Triple Entente Formed

Britain, France, and Russia formalise their alliance — Europe is now divided into two armed camps.

28 June 1914
Assassination of Franz Ferdinand

Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to Austria-Hungary, is assassinated in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip of the Black Hand — the spark that ignites WW1.

28 July 1914
WW1 Begins

Austria-Hungary declares war on Serbia. Within days, the alliance system drags in Russia, Germany, France, and Britain into the war.

4 August 1914
Britain (and Australia) Declare War

Britain declares war on Germany after Germany invades Belgium. Australia, as part of the British Empire, is automatically at war. PM Andrew Fisher pledges Australia's "last man and last shilling."

25 April 1915
ANZAC Landing at Gallipoli

Australian and New Zealand troops land at ANZAC Cove on the Gallipoli Peninsula. They land at the wrong beach and face steep cliffs under heavy Ottoman fire. This date becomes ANZAC Day.

July–November 1916
Battle of the Somme

One of the bloodiest battles in history. On Day 1 alone, the British Army suffered nearly 57,000 casualties. Australian forces are heavily involved later in the battle.

December 1915
Evacuation of Gallipoli

After 8 months of failure, Allied forces secretly evacuate Gallipoli. The evacuation was the best-executed part of the entire campaign. Over 8,700 Australians had died.

October 1916
First Conscription Referendum (Australia)

PM Billy Hughes holds a referendum on introducing conscription. It is defeated 1,087,557 votes to 1,160,033 — Australians narrowly vote NO.

December 1917
Second Conscription Referendum (Australia)

Hughes holds a second referendum — also defeated. Australia remains the only major Allied nation without compulsory military service.

11 November 1918
Armistice — WW1 Ends

Fighting stops at 11:00 am on 11 November 1918. Over 60,000 Australians had been killed.

28 June 1919
Treaty of Versailles Signed

WW1 officially ends. Germany is blamed, fined, and stripped of territory. Australia signs as an independent nation for the first time — a historic moment for Australian sovereignty.

25 April 1916
First Official ANZAC Day

The first anniversary of the Gallipoli landing is observed as ANZAC Day across Australia and New Zealand. It becomes the country's most important day of national remembrance.

Key Concepts Glossary

Learn each definition precisely — you may be asked to define these terms in an exam. Use the search box to find specific terms.

Alliances
Formal agreements between two or more countries to support each other, especially in the event of war. Alliances were a major long-term cause of WW1 — they turned a regional conflict between Austria-Hungary and Serbia into a world war through a chain reaction.
Example: The Triple Alliance between Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy obligated each to defend the others if attacked.
Allies
The countries that opposed the Central Powers in WW1. The main Allied powers were Britain, France, Russia (until 1917), Italy (from 1915), and the USA (from 1917). Australia fought as part of the British Empire and is counted among the Allies.
Example: When Australia went to war in 1914, it fought alongside its British and French allies against Germany and Austria-Hungary.
Anzac Legend
The set of values and qualities believed to have been demonstrated by ANZAC soldiers — especially at Gallipoli. These include courage, endurance, mateship (loyalty to fellow soldiers), larrikinism (healthy disrespect for authority), and a "fair go" ethic. The legend became central to Australian national identity.
Example: The ANZAC legend explains why Gallipoli is remembered as a defining moment for Australia, despite being a military defeat.
Censorship
The suppression or deliberate restriction of information, usually by a government. During WW1, governments censored soldiers' letters home, newspaper reporting, and photographs so the public would not know the full horror of the war — maintaining support for the fighting.
Example: Soldiers' letters describing heavy casualties or criticism of commanders were read by military censors and those sections were blacked out before delivery.
Conscription
Compulsory (legally forced) enrolment into military service. Australia held two referendums on conscription (1916 and 1917) and defeated both — making Australia one of the only Allied nations to rely entirely on volunteers. This deeply divided Australian society.
Example: PM Billy Hughes wanted to introduce conscription to replace battlefield losses, but Australian voters rejected it twice.
Central Powers
The alliance of nations that fought against the Allies in WW1: Germany, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire (Turkey), and Bulgaria. They were called the "Central Powers" because of their central geographic position in Europe.
Example: Australia's enemies — the Central Powers — were led by Germany and included the Ottomans, who fought the ANZACs at Gallipoli.
Imperialism
The policy or practice of extending a country's power and influence by acquiring colonies, controlling other nations' economies, or using military force. Before WW1, European imperial rivalry — especially competition for African and Asian colonies — created intense tension between the major powers.
Example: Germany resented British and French control of vast empires and wanted "a place in the sun" for itself, causing diplomatic crises before 1914.
Nationalism
Strong pride in and devotion to one's nation or ethnic group, often including the desire for political independence. In the Balkans, nationalism among Slavic peoples directly triggered WW1 — particularly Serbian nationalism's threat to the multi-ethnic Austro-Hungarian Empire.
Example: Gavrilo Princip was motivated by Serbian nationalism when he assassinated Franz Ferdinand, believing it would free South Slavic peoples from Austro-Hungarian rule.
No Man's Land
The open, unoccupied and highly dangerous ground between the opposing trench systems on the Western Front. It was typically filled with barbed wire, shell craters, mud, unexploded ordnance, and the bodies of the dead. Advancing across it during an attack ("going over the top") resulted in catastrophic casualties.
Example: On the first day of the Battle of the Somme, thousands of soldiers died crossing No Man's Land, cut down by German machine guns.
Pacifist
A person who believes that war and all forms of violence are morally unjustifiable and that disputes should always be resolved peacefully. During WW1, pacifists in Australia often opposed both the war and conscription. They faced intense social pressure, accusations of cowardice, and sometimes legal persecution.
Example: Many members of the labour movement were pacifists who argued that working-class men were dying to protect the profits of wealthy elites.
Propaganda
Biased or misleading information deliberately created and spread to promote a particular political cause or point of view. WW1 governments used propaganda to recruit soldiers, maintain public morale, demonise the enemy, and discourage opposition to the war. It often involved appeals to emotion, fear, and patriotism.
Example: Recruitment posters showing German soldiers committing atrocities against women and children were designed to provoke outrage and encourage men to enlist.
Stalemate
A situation in which neither side in a conflict can make significant progress or achieve victory. On the Western Front, a military stalemate developed by late 1914 and lasted until 1918 — both sides dug into trench systems that neither could break through, resulting in years of bloody deadlock.
Example: Despite suffering over a million casualties at the Somme and Verdun in 1916, the stalemate on the Western Front barely moved a few kilometres in either direction.
Triple Alliance
The pre-war military alliance (formed 1882) between Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy, in which all three agreed to defend each other if attacked by other great powers. Italy did not honour the alliance when war started in 1914 (claiming it was a defensive alliance only) and actually joined the Allies in 1915.
Example: When Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia in 1914, it expected Germany to support it under the Triple Alliance — and Germany did.
Triple Entente
The pre-war diplomatic agreement (formalised 1907) between France, Russia, and Britain. Unlike the Triple Alliance, it was not a formal military treaty but rather an understanding of mutual support. It formed the core of the Allied powers at the start of WW1.
Example: When Germany declared war on France in 1914, Britain entered the war under the obligations of the Triple Entente and because Germany had violated Belgian neutrality.
Western Front
The main battle zone in Western Europe during WW1, running approximately 700 km from the Belgian coast to Switzerland. It was the site of the war's most devastating fighting — characterised by entrenched positions, stalemate, industrial-scale casualties, and the first large-scale use of new weapons like poison gas, tanks, and aircraft.
Example: Australian forces served on the Western Front from 1916, fighting in major battles including Pozières, Bullecourt, and Hamel.

Short Answer Practice

These are the style of questions that appear in tests and exams. Read the question, think about your answer, then reveal the model A-grade response to compare.

1. Explain TWO long-term causes of World War One. 4 marks
How to answer (4 marks = 2 marks per cause): For each cause, state what it is, then explain HOW it contributed to the war. Do not just define the term — explain its role as a cause.

One long-term cause of WW1 was nationalism. Nationalism is the strong desire of an ethnic group to have its own independent nation. In the Balkans, Serbian nationalism posed a direct threat to Austria-Hungary, which ruled over several Slavic ethnic groups. This tension culminated in the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo on 28 June 1914 by Gavrilo Princip, a Serbian nationalist — which sparked the war.


A second long-term cause was the alliance system. Europe was divided into two armed camps — the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (Britain, France, Russia). These alliances meant that when Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, a chain reaction pulled in the major powers. Russia mobilised to protect Serbia; Germany declared war on Russia; France entered; then Britain entered when Germany violated Belgian neutrality. A local conflict escalated into a world war because of the alliance system.

Why this scores full marks: Each cause is stated clearly, explained in context, and linked to the outbreak of war with specific detail.
2. Describe the conditions that soldiers experienced on the Western Front. 4 marks
How to answer: Describe at least 3–4 specific conditions. Use precise language and specific details — "trench foot," "shell shock," "No Man's Land," etc. Do not be vague.

Soldiers on the Western Front faced appalling physical and psychological conditions. The trenches were waterlogged and muddy, causing a medical condition called trench foot, where feet became infected and rotted from constant moisture. Trenches were infested with enormous rats that fed on the bodies of the dead, and with lice that spread disease. Disease — including dysentery, typhoid, and trench fever — spread rapidly through the unsanitary trenches.


Perhaps the most psychologically damaging experience was the constant artillery bombardment, which caused shell shock — what we now call PTSD. Soldiers suffered trembling, nightmares, inability to function, and loss of speech. When ordered to go "over the top" and cross No Man's Land — the barbed-wire strewn, muddy ground between the trenches — they faced devastating fire from machine guns. At the Battle of the Somme (1916), approximately 57,000 British soldiers became casualties on the first day alone.

Why this scores full marks: Uses specific terms, specific examples including the Battle of the Somme, and addresses both physical and psychological conditions.
3. Explain how Australian soldiers viewed the Gallipoli campaign. Refer to more than one perspective. 5 marks
How to answer: "Perspectives" requires you to show more than one view. Cover initial expectations vs. reality, or compare how different groups of soldiers felt (early enthusiasm, shock on landing, later disillusionment).

Australian soldiers held varying perspectives on the Gallipoli campaign, ranging from initial enthusiasm to subsequent disillusionment.


Initially, many Australians enlisted with excitement, seeing the war as an adventure and an opportunity to serve the British Empire. This enthusiasm reflected the broader patriotism of the time — Australia had only been a federation since 1901 and many felt a strong loyalty to Britain.


However, the reality of the Gallipoli landing on 25 April 1915 was shocking. The ANZACs landed at the wrong beach, facing steep cliffs and immediately coming under heavy fire. Soldiers were confronted with carnage — dead and wounded comrades in the water, chaos, and relentless Ottoman fire from above. Private letters (those not censored) described horror, confusion, and grief.


Despite this, the campaign also generated tremendous bravery and mateship. Soldiers took pride in their resilience and loyalty to one another. Figures like "Simpson and his donkey" (John Simpson Kirkpatrick, who carried wounded soldiers under fire) became famous symbols of ANZAC courage.


As the campaign dragged on through winter with no progress, disillusionment grew. Many soldiers and junior officers felt that British commanders had poorly planned the operation and were sacrificing lives needlessly. This contributed to a view of Australians as distinct from — and in some ways superior to — their British counterparts.

Why this scores full marks: Clearly presents multiple perspectives (enthusiasm, shock, bravery, disillusionment), uses specific examples, and shows how perspectives changed over time.
4. Explain what propaganda is and how it was used during WW1. 4 marks
How to answer: Define propaganda first, then give at least 2–3 specific examples of how it was used. Link propaganda to censorship for bonus marks.

Propaganda is biased or misleading information deliberately created to promote a political cause, influence public opinion, or change people's behaviour.


During WW1, all warring nations used propaganda extensively. Its main purposes included military recruitment — posters showed soldiers as heroes and used guilt and patriotism to encourage men to enlist. Slogans like "Your Country Needs You" targeted men directly. Women were also enlisted as propaganda tools through the white feather campaign, in which women handed white feathers (symbols of cowardice) to men not in uniform, publicly shaming them into enlisting.


Governments also used propaganda to demonise the enemy, portraying Germans as barbaric "Huns" who committed atrocities against innocent civilians — often exaggerating or fabricating events to generate outrage. This kept public support for the war strong, even as casualties mounted.


Propaganda worked hand-in-hand with censorship. While propaganda pushed positive, patriotic messages, censorship blocked negative information — soldiers' letters were read and edited, and newspapers were discouraged from reporting high casualty figures or criticism of commanders.

Why this scores full marks: Clear definition, multiple specific examples (posters, white feathers, demonisation), and the link to censorship demonstrates deeper understanding.
5. How did WW1 change Australia? Use evidence to support your answer. 6 marks
How to answer (6 marks): Cover at least 3 distinct areas of change — national identity, political changes, social changes, economic impact. Use specific evidence (dates, statistics, events).

WW1 fundamentally transformed Australia in several key areas.


National Identity: The war — and particularly the Gallipoli campaign — forged a distinct Australian national identity. The ANZAC legend, celebrating qualities of courage, mateship, and resilience, became the foundation of how Australians saw themselves as a people, distinct from Britain. ANZAC Day (25 April) became Australia's most important national day of remembrance, first observed in 1916.


Political Changes: Australia's relationship with Britain changed significantly. Signing the Treaty of Versailles in 1919 as an independent nation — not just as part of the British Empire — marked an important step toward political sovereignty. Domestically, the conscription debate split the country and the Labor Party, with PM Billy Hughes being expelled from Labor after two failed referendums in 1916 and 1917.


Social Changes: Women's wartime roles challenged traditional gender expectations. Over 2,500 nurses served with the AANS in combat zones, and women worked in munitions factories and transport — roles previously closed to them. Returned soldiers brought home severe physical injuries and psychological trauma (shell shock) that society struggled to support. Anti-German sentiment led to discrimination against German-Australians.

6. Why was the Battle of Gallipoli so significant for Australia, despite being a military failure? 5 marks
How to answer: Acknowledge the military failure, then explain the cultural/national significance. The contrast between military failure and cultural success is the key to a high mark here.

The Gallipoli campaign (April–December 1915) was clearly a military failure. The Allies failed to capture the Dardanelles or Constantinople, and the campaign ended with a full evacuation in December 1915. Approximately 8,700 Australians were killed and 19,441 were wounded — enormous losses for a nation of fewer than 5 million people.


Despite this failure, Gallipoli became the most significant event in Australian national history for several reasons.


First, it gave birth to the ANZAC legend — the belief that Australian soldiers demonstrated extraordinary and uniquely Australian qualities: courage, endurance, digger spirit, and above all mateship. This gave Australians a shared national story and a sense of pride distinct from their British identity.


Second, it created ANZAC Day (25 April) — now Australia's most sacred national day, observed with dawn services across the country and around the world. More Australians now attend ANZAC Day services than at any previous time in history, demonstrating the enduring significance of Gallipoli.


Third, the campaign influenced Australia's developing sense of national independence. Many Australians felt that British commanders had mismanaged the campaign and wasted Australian lives. This strengthened the view that Australia should act as a more independent nation — a view that ultimately led to Australia signing the Treaty of Versailles independently in 1919.

Why this scores full marks: Clearly acknowledges the military failure, then systematically explains THREE reasons for its national significance, using specific details throughout.
